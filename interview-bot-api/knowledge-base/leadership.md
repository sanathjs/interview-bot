# Leadership Stories (STAR Format)

## Leadership — Leading the Team as Lead Software Engineer at Ingenio

> Promoted to Lead Software Engineer at Ingenio, owning architecture and delivery of the **Keen platform** — a high-traffic consumer app with multiple third-party integrations.

**Situation**

At Ingenio, I was promoted to **Lead Software Engineer / Team Lead**, overseeing development of Keen — a high-traffic consumer app with multiple third-party integrations.

**Task**

Lead the engineering team, **make architectural decisions**, ensure delivery quality, and coordinate with designers, product managers, and stakeholders.

**Action**

- Established **coding standards** and led **architecture design sessions**.
- Broke down complex features into **manageable tasks** for the team.
- Personally handled the **most critical integrations**.
- Set up **CI/CD pipelines** with TeamCity and Azure DevOps to streamline deployments.

**Result**

> Delivered a stable, scalable platform integrating **Zinrelo, Iterable, Zendesk, and Contentstack** while maintaining team productivity and code quality.

## Leadership — Mentoring Junior Developers

> My job as Lead is not only to ship — it's to make the next generation of engineers on my team able to ship without me.

**Situation**

As Lead Software Engineer, I was responsible for the growth of **junior team members** who were new to enterprise .NET and cloud development.

**Task**

Bring junior developers up to speed with our tech stack, coding standards, and architectural patterns **without slowing down project delivery**.

**Action**

- Conducted **regular code reviews** with detailed, written feedback.
- **Pair-programmed** on complex features.
- Shared best practices in **team sessions**.
- **Documented architectural decisions** so the team could learn from them.
- **Published technical articles** to contribute to the broader developer community — *NoSQL / MongoDB for Beginners*, *Deploying Node.js to Azure*.

**Result**

> Junior developers gained confidence and independence faster, **reducing review cycles** and allowing the team to take on more complex features.

## Leadership — Technical Decision: Microservice vs Monolith

> Pragmatism over dogma. Microservices where independent scaling matters; monolith where it doesn't.

**Situation**

When designing solutions at Ingenio, I regularly had to **decide between microservice and monolithic patterns** depending on the project context.

**Task**

Pick the architectural pattern that balances **scalability**, **team size**, and **delivery speed**.

**Action**

- Assessed **team size**, **deployment complexity**, and the **nature of each domain**.
- For Keen, I took a **hybrid approach**:
  - **Microservices** where independent scaling was needed (e.g. advisor matching, billing).
  - **Monolithic** for parts where the operational overhead of microservices wasn't justified.

**Result**

> Architecture supported platform growth **without over-engineering**, keeping the team fast.

## Leadership — How I Define Good Leadership

> A good lead writes code alongside the team, not just in meetings.

**What good technical leadership means to me**

- **Make clear architectural decisions** — and communicate the *why* behind them.
- **Protect the team from distractions** — fight to keep their focus time intact.
- **Invest in people's growth** — every review, every pairing session, every doc.
- **Stay technical** — keep shipping code so you don't drift away from the reality the team is in.
