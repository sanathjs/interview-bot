# Leadership Stories (STAR Format)

## Story 1 — Leading a Team as Lead Software Engineer at Ingenio
Situation: At Ingenio, I was promoted to Lead Software Engineer / Team Lead, overseeing development
of the Keen platform — a high-traffic consumer app with multiple third-party integrations.
Task: My responsibility was to lead the engineering team, make architectural decisions, ensure
delivery quality, and coordinate with designers, product managers, and stakeholders.
Action: I established coding standards, led architecture design sessions, broke down complex features
into manageable tasks for the team, and personally handled the most critical integrations. I also
set up CI/CD pipelines with TeamCity and Azure DevOps to streamline deployments.
Result: Delivered a stable, scalable platform integrating multiple third-party tools (Zinrelo,
Iterable, Zendesk, Content Stack) while maintaining team productivity and code quality.

## Story 2 — Mentoring Junior Developers
Situation: As Lead Software Engineer, I was responsible for the growth of junior team members
who were new to enterprise .NET and cloud development.
Task: I needed to bring junior developers up to speed with our tech stack, coding standards,
and architectural patterns without slowing down project delivery.
Action: I conducted regular code reviews with detailed feedback, pair-programmed on complex features,
shared best practices in team sessions, and documented architectural decisions so the team could
learn from them. I also published technical articles (e.g., NoSQL/MongoDB for Beginners,
Deploying Node.js to Azure) to contribute to the broader developer community.
Result: Junior developers gained confidence and independence faster, reducing the number of
review cycles needed and allowing the team to take on more complex features.

## Story 3 — Making a Technical Decision — Microservice vs Monolithic Architecture
Situation: When designing solutions at Ingenio, I regularly had to decide between microservice
and monolithic architectural patterns depending on the project context.
Task: Evaluate the right architectural pattern for the platform to balance scalability,
team size, and delivery speed.
Action: I assessed the team size, deployment complexity, and the nature of the platform's domains.
For the Keen platform, I used a pragmatic approach — applying microservices where independent
scaling was needed (e.g., advisor matching, billing) and keeping other parts monolithic to
reduce operational overhead.
Result: The architecture supported the platform's growth while keeping the team able to
move quickly without over-engineering.

## How I Define Good Leadership
Good technical leadership is about enabling your team to do their best work. It means making
clear architectural decisions, communicating the "why" behind them, protecting the team from
distractions, and investing in people's growth. A good lead writes code alongside the team,
not just in meetings.
